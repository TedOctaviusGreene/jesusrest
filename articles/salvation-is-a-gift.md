# Salvation Is a Gift You Cannot Earn or Lose

*The foundation of the whole salvation system — and the one part everything else depends on.*

**The Salvation System — Part 1 of Eight**

This article is about one part of a single, connected system of salvation, and it begins with the part that everything else depends on. Here is the whole system in one sentence, so that the foundation can be seen in its place: God's salvation is a gift that a person cannot earn and cannot lose; the person who receives that gift becomes a son or a daughter of God; a son or daughter who lives by the flesh is disciplined but never disowned; a son or daughter who yields to Christ bears Christ's own fruit and is rewarded for it; and at no point in the entire system can anyone boast.

Every other part of that system rests on the first part. If salvation could be earned, then a saved person would have something to boast about. If salvation could be lost, then a saved person would live in fear of losing it. So before anything else in the system can be taught, this one claim has to be settled and proven: salvation is a gift, it is received by faith, and it can be neither earned nor lost. That single claim is what this article will establish, directly from Scripture.

## A gift is not earned

Eternal life is not wages. Eternal life is a gift, and a gift is not paid for. Paul says this in the plainest words the New Testament has on the subject:

> For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God: not of works, lest any man should boast.
>
> — Ephesians 2:8–9 KJV

Read what Paul guards in that sentence. Salvation is built so that boasting is impossible — "lest any man should boast." That outcome is not an accident of grace; it is the design of grace. The moment a person's salvation depends on something that the person produced, that person has a reason to be proud, and God will not build His house on human pride. Paul says the same thing again from the opposite side, by contrasting wages with a gift:

> For the wages of sin is death; but the gift of God is eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord.
>
> — Romans 6:23 KJV

Death is the wage. Death is what a person earns. Eternal life is the gift. Eternal life is what God gives. Wages are paid for work done; a gift is handed over freely. Paul keeps the two words on opposite sides of that verse on purpose, so that no reader can quietly turn the gift back into a wage.

## A gift is not taken back

What God gives, God does not take back. Paul states this as a fixed rule about how God deals with people:

> For the gifts and calling of God are without repentance.
>
> — Romans 11:29 KJV

"Without repentance" means that God does not change His mind about the gift once He has given it. And the Lord Jesus made the same promise in the strongest words He could have chosen:

> And I give unto them eternal life; and they shall never perish, neither shall any man pluck them out of My hand. My Father, which gave them Me, is greater than all; and no man is able to pluck them out of My Father's hand.
>
> — John 10:28–29 KJV

Notice that the Lord Jesus does not say His people are hard to lose. He says His people cannot be lost. No one is able to take them out of His hand, and no one is able to take them out of the Father's hand. The same promise removes even the fear of being condemned later:

> Verily, verily, I say unto you, He that heareth My word, and believeth on Him that sent Me, hath everlasting life, and shall not come into condemnation; but is passed from death unto life.
>
> — John 5:24 KJV

A person who has believed already *has* everlasting life — present tense, already given — and that person "shall not come into condemnation." The verdict has already passed from death to life. There is no later trial in which that verdict is reopened.

> **Death is the wage a person earns. Eternal life is the gift God gives.**

## The gift covers even your worst day

The promise is strong enough to cover the day a believer's own faith goes cold. Paul writes about exactly that case:

> If we are faithless, He remains faithful; He cannot deny Himself.
>
> — 2 Timothy 2:13 NKJV

That single line is one of the kindest sentences in the Bible. When a believer fails God, God does not become unfaithful in return to match the believer's failure. God cannot deny Himself — and the believer is now part of what God will not deny. The reason the gift holds even on a faithless day is that the gift was sealed at the beginning, the moment the person believed:

> In whom ye also trusted, after that ye heard the word of truth, the gospel of your salvation: in whom also after that ye believed, ye were sealed with that Holy Spirit of promise, which is the earnest of our inheritance.
>
> — Ephesians 1:13–14 KJV

The believer was sealed with the Holy Spirit, and that sealing is called "the earnest of our inheritance" — a down payment that guarantees the rest. A seal is not a thing a person earns and not a thing a person can break. God placed the seal, and God keeps it.

## Why the foundation has to be settled first

So the first part of the system is firm beyond argument: salvation is received, never achieved; and once it is received, it cannot be lost. That settled foundation is what makes the rest of the system possible, and it is worth saying plainly why.

Because salvation is a gift and not a wage, no saved person will ever be able to boast of being saved. Because the gift cannot be lost, no son or daughter of God has to live in fear of losing it. The next parts of the system — that the saved person is a begotten child of God, that a wandering child is disciplined rather than disowned, that a yielded child is rewarded, and that the child is finally born into glory — all stand on this one settled fact. Take this foundation away, and the rest of the system collapses into either pride or fear. Leave this foundation in place, and everything else can be built on it without fear and without boasting.

Salvation is a gift. A person receives it by faith. A person can neither earn it nor lose it. That is where the system begins.
