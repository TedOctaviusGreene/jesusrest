# You Are New, and You Still Suffer

*Why the sickness, the struggle, and even the grave were never evidence against the new life in you — but exactly what it predicted.*

There is a kind of person this is written for, and you may be standing in his shoes right now.

He believed. Not casually — he gave himself over. He came to the end of running his own life and handed it to Christ, and he was told something staggering in return: that he was now a new creation, that Christ Himself had come to live in him, that the old self was gone and a new life had begun. He believed it. And then he got the diagnosis. Or the marriage cracked. Or the depression came back on a gray morning and sat on his chest like it always had. Or he buried someone who loved God more than he did. Or he simply noticed that the same anger, the same appetite, the same smallness he thought had been crucified was still very much alive and answering to his name.

And a quiet, terrible question formed: *If Christ is my life, why does my life look like this? If I am a new creation, why am I still sick, still struggling, still going to die like everyone else?*

That question is not a sign of weak faith. It is one of the most honest questions a believing person can ask, and most teaching runs from it. We are not going to run from it. We are going to walk straight into it, because the claim that Christ lives in you is either strong enough to survive your worst day or it isn't worth making. It is strong enough. But you have to see how, and why, and that takes some patience. So stay with it. By the end, the very things that look like they disprove the new life will turn out to be the proof that the whole picture is true.

## First, let's say the doubt out loud at full strength

It would be dishonest to rush past how reasonable the dark version of this feels, so let's give it its full weight before answering it.

Here is the doubt: maybe "you are a new creation, Christ lives in you" is just beautiful language — the kind of thing people say to feel better, that comforts in church and dissolves in a hospital room. Maybe the believers who glow about the new life in them simply haven't been hit hard enough yet. Maybe, when you look honestly, the Christian in the cancer ward dies on the same timeline as the man who believed nothing, and that fact ought to mean something. Maybe the whole thing is a story we tell over a reality that hasn't actually changed.

If you have felt the edge of that, you are not faithless and you are not alone. The honest answer is not to deny the data. The honest answer is that there is a fuller picture in which every one of those hard facts has a place — and once you see the structure, the picture doesn't just survive the facts. It needs them.

## What the claim actually is

Before we test it, we have to state it precisely, because most people are carrying a watered-down version that was always going to break.

The claim is not that you have been improved. It is not that God gave your old self a tune-up, some better habits, a little help in tough moments. The claim is far more radical than that, and far better. It is that something genuinely new came into existence when you were joined to Christ — a new self whose very life is Christ Himself living in you.

> Therefore if any man be in Christ, he is a new creature: old things are passed away; behold, all things are become new.
> — 2 Corinthians 5:17

> I am crucified with Christ: nevertheless I live; yet not I, but Christ liveth in me.
> — Galatians 2:20

> Christ in you, the hope of glory.
> — Colossians 1:27

> When Christ, who is our life, shall appear, then shall ye also appear with Him in glory.
> — Colossians 3:4

Read those slowly. Not Christ *assisting* your life. Christ *as* your life. This is the spine of everything: the new creation is not a polished version of you. It is a new person whose source of life is Another, living inside you, sharing with you everything that is His. His righteousness is now yours. His standing before the Father is now yours. His victory is now yours. You did not earn it and you cannot lose it by failing, because it was never anchored in your performance — it is anchored in Him.

And there is a word Scripture uses for this arrangement that holds the whole mystery together, so hold onto it: *betrothal.*

> For I am jealous over you with godly jealousy: for I have espoused you to one husband, that I may present you as a chaste virgin to Christ.
> — 2 Corinthians 11:2

In the world that sentence was written into, betrothal was not a casual engagement. It was legally binding. The moment you were betrothed, you belonged to him — fully, really, by covenant. You took his name. You were his. And yet the wedding, the consummation, the being-brought-home into the fullness of the life together, was still ahead. Truly his now. Not yet home. Remember that. It is the key that unlocks the entire paradox.

## Now the hard part: the facts that look like they disprove it

Here is where honest thinking begins, because a claim this large has to account for the things that seem to contradict it. If we hide the inconvenient facts, we are not teaching — we are selling. So let's lay every hard fact on the table, the very ones that made you ask the question in the first place:

The new creation still sins. *"If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves, and the truth is not in us"* (1 John 1:8).

The new creation still fights a war it doesn't always feel like it's winning. *"For the flesh lusteth against the Spirit, and the Spirit against the flesh: and these are contrary the one to the other: so that ye cannot do the things that ye would"* (Galatians 5:17).

The new creation still gets sick. Still suffers. Still grieves. Sometimes prays for relief and does not receive it. And one hundred percent of the time, the body of the new creation grows old and dies, exactly like everyone else's.

If the claim were "Christ lives in me, therefore I am healthy, victorious in every moment, and exempt from the grave," then every sickbed and every funeral would be hard evidence against it. And so we have to ask the real question: does the new-creation claim actually predict a perfect, painless, undying life? Or does it predict something else — something that has room for all of this?

It predicts something else. And the structure that makes room is the single most important thing in this entire article.

## The key: already, and not yet

Watch this carefully, because once you see it you cannot unsee it, and it explains nearly everything that confused you.

Scripture makes two claims about the new creation at the same time, on purpose. It says the new life is a present, finished fact — already true, already yours. And it says the completion of that new life is still in the future — not yet arrived. Both. In the same breath. By the same authors. Deliberately.

> And not only they, but ourselves also, which have the firstfruits of the Spirit, even we ourselves groan within ourselves, waiting for the adoption, to wit, the redemption of our body.
> — Romans 8:23

Stop on that. We *already* have the Spirit — the down payment, the firstfruits, the guarantee. And we are *still waiting* for the adoption, which Paul defines precisely: the redemption of our body. So we are already adopted (he says so eight verses earlier, Romans 8:15), and we are still waiting for the adoption. That is not a contradiction. That is the entire shape of the Christian life. The new creation is real now in the spirit; it has not yet reached the body.

Which is exactly why Paul can say:

> For which cause we faint not; but though our outward man perish, yet the inward man is renewed day by day.
> — 2 Corinthians 4:16

Read what that sentence is actually claiming. At the very same time, by the same process of grace, the inner self is being renewed and the outer self — the body — is perishing. The decay of your body is not a sign the renewal failed. The renewal and the decay are happening together, by design. Your body wearing out, getting sick, and finally dying is not evidence against the new creation. It is precisely what the new-creation picture predicts during this stretch of the story: a new life that is already fully real on the inside, housed for now in a body that has not yet been redeemed.

This is the betrothal again. You are truly His — that is settled, legal, done. But you have not yet been brought home. The body you live in now is the body of the betrothal, not the wedding. The redemption that is already finished in your spirit has simply not yet been applied to your flesh. And Scripture tells you the exact moment it will be:

> Behold, I shew you a mystery; We shall not all sleep, but we shall all be changed, in a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last trump… and the dead shall be raised incorruptible, and we shall be changed.
> — 1 Corinthians 15:51–52

> Who shall change our vile body, that it may be fashioned like unto His glorious body.
> — Philippians 3:21

The resurrection is the wedding. That is when the new creation finally fills the body too. Until then, you live in the gap — fully His, not yet home — and the gap is not a flaw in the promise. The gap *is* the promise, honestly told.

## "We win every battle because He already won" — true, with one careful line

Now we can handle the question of victory honestly, because people get hurt here in a very specific way.

It is gloriously true that the outcome is settled. The war is won, and it was won by Him, not by your effort:

> Nay, in all these things we are more than conquerors through Him that loved us.
> — Romans 8:37

> For whatsoever is born of God overcometh the world: and this is the victory that overcometh the world, even our faith.
> — 1 John 5:4

So when you yield to Christ, you are not straining toward a victory you might win. You are standing in a victory already won, reminding yourself whose you are. You surrender your own will — not because you're waiting for a feeling to change, not waiting for the flesh to suddenly want different things, but because by faith you already know you are in Him and His life is yours. That is the right posture, and it is the secret to genuine rest.

But here is the careful line, because this is where sincere people get crushed: "the war is won" is not the same as "I will perform flawlessly in every moment." Those are two different claims, and only the first one is promised. The war's outcome is certain. The individual skirmishes of the daily walk still include real stumbling — Scripture says plainly that we still sin (1 John 1:8), and even the greatest believer who ever wrote described himself, at the height of his faith, still pressing and still disciplining himself (Philippians 3:12; 1 Corinthians 9:27). So if anyone tells you the new creation means you'll never fall again, never snap at someone you love, never lose a battle with an old habit — they have overstated the promise, and the overstatement will wound you. Because the first time you do fall, you'll conclude Christ failed you, when in fact the claim was simply too big.

> **You fight from a won field, not toward one.**

Here is the precise truth: the victory is total and it is yours, and your moment-by-moment experience of it is not a perfect scoreboard. And when you stumble, you have not lost the war — you have stumbled inside a war already decided, with an Advocate who has not moved: *"And if any man sin, we have an advocate with the Father, Jesus Christ the righteous"* (1 John 2:1).

## The big question: why do believers suffer at all?

This is the question underneath all the others, and it deserves the most care, because the wrong answer here is not just incorrect — it is cruel, and it has wounded more sincere believers than almost any error in the church.

The cruel answer goes like this: if you are suffering, you must have done something. There must be hidden sin. God is punishing you, or you lack the faith to be free of it. This is the oldest bad theology there is, and Scripture names it and rejects it. So before we sort out the real reasons, let us be clear: suffering in a believer's life is not a single thing with a single cause. Scripture lays out several distinct reasons a person walking with God may suffer, and you cannot collapse them into one. Let's walk through each, so you can stop torturing yourself with the wrong question.

### 1. It may be the loving discipline of a Father with His child.

This one is real, and Scripture does not soften it: *"For whom the Lord loveth He chasteneth, and scourgeth every son whom He receiveth"* (Hebrews 12:6). The point to grasp is in the word *son.* This is not the anger of a judge against a criminal. It is the correction of a Father toward a child He fully owns and will never disown. The same passage says it is unpleasant — *"no chastening for the present seemeth to be joyous, but grievous"* (Hebrews 12:11) — and yet it is proof of sonship, not evidence against it.

It can even be severe. Some believers in Corinth were sick, and some had even died, as discipline — and notice carefully how Paul frames it:

> For this cause many are weak and sickly among you, and many sleep. For if we would judge ourselves, we should not be judged. But when we are judged, we are chastened of the Lord, that we should not be condemned with the world.
> — 1 Corinthians 11:30–32

Read that last line until it sinks in. The chastening exists specifically so that they will *not* be condemned. The discipline is the opposite of condemnation. It is the mark of a child being kept, not a criminal being sentenced.

### 2. It may be the natural consequence of something actually done — without that meaning your standing is in danger.

Here we have to hold two things that people constantly confuse. The penalty for sin — the eternal debt every person owes — has been fully and finally paid by Christ. That is settled, and nothing you do can reopen it. But the earthly consequences of what you actually do are a different matter, and grace does not always erase them. *"Be not deceived; God is not mocked: for whatsoever a man soweth, that shall he also reap"* (Galatians 6:7).

The clearest picture is the king who sinned grievously, confessed, and was forgiven on the spot — *"The Lord also hath put away thy sin; thou shalt not die"* — and yet was told in the same breath that the child would die and that violence would follow his house (2 Samuel 12:13–14). Fully forgiven. The eternal debt cancelled. And the earthly consequences still unfolded. So if your present suffering traces back to something you did, that does not mean you are condemned, and it does not mean grace failed. It means you are forgiven and still living through the wake of an action — and even that wake is held in the hands of a Father who is working it toward your good.

### 3. It may be refining — the proving and strengthening of genuine faith.

This is not punishment at all, and confusing it for punishment will rob you. *"That the trial of your faith, being much more precious than of gold that perisheth, though it be tried with fire, might be found unto praise and honour and glory at the appearing of Jesus Christ"* (1 Peter 1:7). *"Knowing this, that the trying of your faith worketh patience"* (James 1:3). Fire on gold is not the gold's enemy; it burns away what was never really gold and leaves what is. Some of your hardest seasons are not God against you — they are God proving, in the only way it can be proven, that what He put in you is real.

### 4. It may be the honor of sharing in Christ's own sufferings.

This one almost no one expects, and it reframes everything. Sometimes a believer suffers not as punishment and not even as refining, but as a participation in the very sufferings of Christ — and Scripture treats this as a privilege, not a penalty. *"And if children, then heirs; heirs of God, and joint-heirs with Christ; if so be that we suffer with Him, that we may be also glorified together"* (Romans 8:17). *"That I may know Him, and the power of His resurrection, and the fellowship of His sufferings"* (Philippians 3:10). When you suffer for righteousness, or simply suffer while belonging to Him, you are not being cast out. You are being drawn into the deepest fellowship there is with the One whose life is now yours.

### 5. It may have no disclosed reason at all — and be entirely for God's glory.

This is the one that breaks the cruel theology completely, so weigh it. When the disciples saw a man born blind, they asked the very question this whole article is built around — who sinned, this man or his parents? — and assumed the suffering had to trace to someone's fault. Jesus rejected the premise outright: *"Neither hath this man sinned, nor his parents: but that the works of God should be made manifest in him"* (John 9:3). The suffering had nothing to do with sin and everything to do with a purpose the man could not have guessed.

And then there is the whole long account of the most righteous man of his time, struck with catastrophe after catastrophe for reasons he was never told — and whose friends insisted, with great confidence and great cruelty, that he must have sinned to deserve it. At the end, it is not the sufferer who is rebuked. It is the friends: *"My wrath is kindled against thee… for ye have not spoken of me the thing that is right"* (Job 42:7). The men who explained a good man's suffering as punishment for hidden sin were the ones God was angry with.

So here is the synthesis you can actually live by. When suffering comes, you are permitted to ask, honestly and without panic, *"Father, is there something here for me to see?"* That question is healthy. But you are not permitted — and you are not required — to render a verdict. You may not conclude "this proves God is against me," or "this proves there is hidden sin in me," or "this proves I didn't believe hard enough." Discernment is allowed. A verdict is forbidden, because you do not have the information to render one, and Scripture shows that the people who confidently rendered it were wrong every time. In most cases you will not be told which of these reasons is operating, and you do not need to know — because the right response to every one of them is exactly the same: draw near to the Father, trust Him, and refuse to read the suffering as a sign of rejection.

## "But if I had more faith, wouldn't I be healed?"

This question deserves its own answer, because it is one of the most common sources of secret torment in believers, and the answer is clear and freeing.

The definitive case is the greatest missionary in the record, who carried some painful affliction he called a thorn, and who asked God to remove it — not once, but three times, in earnest. He was not healed. And the reason given was not a lack of faith. It was the opposite — it was God's deliberate purpose:

> And He said unto me, My grace is sufficient for thee: for my strength is made perfect in weakness.
> — 2 Corinthians 12:9

The most faith-filled man imaginable, asking three times, deliberately not healed, for a purpose higher than his comfort. And it was not an isolated case. The same man left a co-worker behind *"sick"* because he could not heal him (2 Timothy 4:20). He told a young protégé to take a little wine for his stomach and his *"often infirmities"* rather than simply commanding healing (1 Timothy 5:23). A beloved companion was *"sick nigh unto death,"* and his recovery is described as God's mercy, not as a foregone conclusion of sufficient faith (Philippians 2:27). The apostolic circle — men of extraordinary faith — were not uniformly healed of everything.

And remember what was settled before: it was never the *size* of your faith that mattered. Faith the size of a mustard seed — the smallest seed there is — is enough, because the power was never in the strength of your believing but in the One believed in (Luke 17:6). Which means a lack of healing cannot be diagnosed as a lack of faith, because the quantity of faith was never the lever in the first place. You may absolutely ask for healing — you should; Paul asked. But when the answer is "My grace is sufficient," that is not God failing you for want of belief. That is God choosing, for reasons of His own glory and your eventual good, to be your strength inside the weakness rather than removing it. The healing of every disease is coming. It is just scheduled for the wedding, not the betrothal.

## "Then isn't this life kind of pointless?" — the most pitiable verse

There is a verse that surfaces for almost everyone who thinks this through honestly, and it sounds at first like despair, but it is actually the answer to the despair. Listen to it, but listen to one word in particular:

> If in this life only we have hope in Christ, we are of all men most miserable.
> — 1 Corinthians 15:19

The word is *only.* Paul is not saying this life is pointless. He is saying something far more pointed: *if* this life were all there is — if there were no resurrection waiting — *then* a Christian, who suffers and serves and dies with nothing on the other side, would be the most pitiable person alive. He is granting the full force of the despair you feel, and then he turns it into the single most thunderous argument in all his writing:

> But now is Christ risen from the dead, and become the firstfruits of them that slept.
> — 1 Corinthians 15:20

There it is. The resurrection is precisely the thing that rescues this life from pointlessness. The reason a life of faith makes sense even when it is full of suffering is that this life is not "only." There is a wedding coming. And in light of that, the entire weight of present suffering is recalculated:

> For our light affliction, which is but for a moment, worketh for us a far more exceeding and eternal weight of glory.
> — 2 Corinthians 4:17

Notice he calls it *light affliction* and *but for a moment* — and this from a man who was beaten, shipwrecked, imprisoned, and finally executed. It was not light in itself. It was light *compared* to what it was producing and to what was coming. That is the recalibration the resurrection makes possible. The ache you feel — that it all seems pointless and joyless in the present condition — is not a faithless feeling. It is the correct feeling about a life with no resurrection. The resurrection is the answer to it.

## So how do I actually live in the gap?

Everything above leads to one practical instruction, and it is the hinge the whole Christian life turns on. If the new creation is real on the inside and the body has not yet caught up, then the entire question of your peace comes down to this: which one are you looking at?

> While we look not at the things which are seen, but at the things which are not seen: for the things which are seen are temporal; but the things which are not seen are eternal.
> — 2 Corinthians 4:18

> Set your affection on things above, not on things on the earth. For ye are dead, and your life is hid with Christ in God.
> — Colossians 3:2–3

Here is the discipline, and it is the opposite of denial. You do not pretend the suffering isn't real. You refuse to let it be the thing you build your identity and your peace on. Your fleshly condition — the diagnosis, the struggle, the grief, the slow wearing-out of the body — is real data about your body. It is simply not the truth about you. It is real, but it is temporary. It is real, but it is not the verdict. And it gets no vote on the question of who you are in Christ.

This is worth saying carefully, because some teaching pretends suffering is an illusion, and that is a lie that shatters the moment real pain arrives. The thorn was real. Paul never said it wasn't; he said grace was sufficient for it. So do not gaslight yourself into calling your pain unreal. Call it what it actually is: real, and temporary, and not the verdict. The moment you take your peace from the unseen — from the finished fact of Christ in you — rather than from the seen, you discover a peace that your circumstances cannot reach, because it was never built on your circumstances in the first place.

## A few more questions, answered plainly

### Does this mean I shouldn't pray for healing or relief?

No. Ask. Paul asked three times. Jesus invited every kind of asking. You bring the request honestly — and then you trust the answer, even when the answer is "My grace is sufficient," because that answer is not a denial of love but a different expression of it.

### If I'm a new creation, why do I still want to sin?

Because the new creation lives for now in a body that has not yet been redeemed, and the pull of the flesh comes from that not-yet-redeemed body, not from the new self. The wanting is not the real you any more than a fever is the real you. The flesh will war until the redemption of the body (Romans 8:23; Galatians 5:17), and then it will be silent forever. The pull is not proof you were never changed. It is proof you are living in the gap.

### How do I know whether my suffering is discipline, or consequence, or refining, or fellowship, or simply life?

Usually you won't know for certain, and that is fine, because the right response to all of them is identical: draw near to the Father, search your heart honestly without panic, trust Him, and refuse to read the pain as rejection. You are not required to diagnose it. You are only required to trust the One holding it.

### Is the death of my body a defeat?

No. It is the last enemy, and it is an enemy already defeated. *"The last enemy that shall be destroyed is death… O death, where is thy sting?… thanks be to God, which giveth us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ"* (1 Corinthians 15:26, 55, 57). For the person in Christ, the death of the body is not the end of the new creation — it is the doorway through which the new creation finally goes home. It is the betrothed at last being brought to the wedding.

## Where this lands

Put the whole picture together and the thing that looked like a contradiction turns out to be a coherent and beautiful design.

You are a new creation. That is not poetry and it is not positive thinking — it is a finished fact, accomplished the moment you were joined to Christ. Your life is now His life, your righteousness is His righteousness, your victory is His victory, and none of it depends on your performance or can be undone by your failures. That is already true, fully, today.

And you are still flesh. You will struggle, you may suffer, you might not be delivered from every hard thing in this life, and your body will one day die. None of that is evidence against the new life in you. All of it is exactly what the picture predicted for this stretch of the story — the gap between the betrothal and the wedding, between the down payment of the Spirit and the redemption of the body. The suffering is real. It is also temporary, it is also not the verdict, and it gets no authority to tell you who you are.

So the lesson, the thing to carry out of all of this, is simple even though the road to it was long: do not build your peace on your current condition, not for a single moment, because your current condition is true about your body and false about you. Build it on Christ, who is your life — unseen, unshakeable, and already entirely yours.

> *You are betrothed to Him. You are truly His now.*
> *The current state is not the truth — the current state is the waiting.*
> *And the One you are waiting for is already living in you,*
> *sharing His own life with you the whole way home.*
