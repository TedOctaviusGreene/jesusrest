# Nobody Gets Away. Nobody Stays Broken.

*The hardest question anyone can ask about God is not why good people have nice things. It is what happens to the person who destroyed a child — and how that child is ever made whole again. The Bible's answer is more exact, and more fair, than we dared hope.*

There is an easy version of the question, and there is a version that keeps people awake. The easy version asks why the comfortable prosper while the decent struggle — and that one mostly dissolves the moment you notice that prosperity was never God's report card. “He maketh His sun to rise on the evil and on the good” (Matthew 5:45); the rain falls on everyone. Wealth is not a medal, and hardship is not a sentence.

But there is a harder version, and it does not dissolve. Somewhere, right now, a child is being destroyed — and someone is doing it. So the real question has two edges, and both of them cut. **What happens to the one who did it? And how is the one it was done to ever made whole?** A life can be taken in a moment. No human power can give it back. That is the question religion usually answers badly, and the atheist is right to demand a better answer than the ones she has been handed. So let the Bible give its own.

## First: nobody gets away

The fear underneath the question is that the cruel simply escape — that they hurt whom they please and then die, and that is the end of it, with no bill ever coming due. The Bible's answer to that fear is total. There is no escape: not through cleverness, not through power, not even through death.

> “Be not deceived; God is not mocked: for whatsoever a man soweth, that shall he also reap.”
> — Galatians 6:7

Death is not the exit the guilty imagine it to be. It is the doorway to the reckoning. “It is appointed unto men once to die, but after this the judgment” (Hebrews 9:27) — and that judgment misses nothing, because the books that are opened there contain everything (Revelation 20:12). And it is not a single blunt verdict dropped on everyone the same. The Bible is strikingly exact that justice is *proportional* — weighed to the very measure of what was done and what was known. Jesus said the one who knew better and did evil would be “beaten with many stripes,” and the one who sinned in ignorance “with few” (Luke 12:47–48). The instinct that the destroyer of a child must not be measured by the same rod as a petty thief is not sentiment the Bible has to correct. It is the Bible's own teaching.

And the one who was wronged does not have to become the executioner. The scales are taken out of their trembling hands by Someone stronger and more just: “Vengeance is Mine; I will repay, saith the Lord” (Romans 12:19). Justice that seems absent in the moment has not been cancelled. It has been moved to a court that cannot be bribed, outrun, or escaped.

## “But doesn't God just forgive them?”

This is the objection that feels like an insult to every victim, so we will not flinch from it. Christianity claims that grace can reach even the worst — that a man who shed innocent blood, even one who passed his own children through fire, could turn and be heard (2 Chronicles 33:6, 12–13); that a man who hunted believers to their deaths could become an apostle (1 Timothy 1:13–15). To a heart aching for a destroyed child, that can sound monstrous: *he says sorry, and walks into paradise clean?*

No. That is not what the Bible says, and the difference is everything. There are two separate debts, and people collapse them into one.

The first debt is the one *every* human owes simply for being a sinner at all — the eternal death penalty. That debt, and only that one, can be borne by Another. When Christ takes it, a person is rescued from eternal death. This is salvation, and it is a gift, not a wage.

The second debt is entirely different: it is what you *did*. And the Bible never says grace erases it. Look closely at the clearest case in Scripture. When King David's sin was exposed, the prophet told him, “The LORD also hath put away thy sin; thou shalt not die” — the death penalty, lifted. And in the very same breath: the child would die, and “the sword shall never depart from thine house” (2 Samuel 12:13–14). Forgiven the penalty. Not exempt from the consequence. The deed was still reckoned, fully, in his own life.

And lest anyone think this applies only to the unsaved, hear it said directly to believers: “He that doeth wrong shall receive for the wrong which he hath done: and there is no respect of persons” (Colossians 3:25). Every person, saved or not, gives an account “whether it be good or bad” (2 Corinthians 5:10).

> The millstone does not vanish when a guilty man kneels. Grace lifts the eternal sentence. It does not buy him out of justice for the act.

So picture the thing honestly. If the one who destroyed a child never turns to God, he bears both debts — eternal death *and* the full, exact reckoning for what he did. If he genuinely turns, the eternal sentence is borne by Christ — but he will still answer, completely and proportionally, for the deed. There is no door anywhere in Scripture through which a person walks *clean* while a child's blood goes unaddressed. God does not wink at it. Saved or lost, the act is met by justice. No one gets away.

And lest anyone fear the reverse — that the victim, or the multitudes who never knew this God, are simply forgotten — the same exact fairness runs the other way. No one is condemned for a chance they never had; the Bible describes an offer of grace extended, in its own time, to every person who ever lived. The God who lets no guilt slip through also lets no one fall through the cracks unoffered. That is the larger picture, laid out in full elsewhere; here it is enough to know that the justice cuts cleanly in both directions.

## Now: nobody stays broken

But punishment, even perfect punishment, is only half of fairness — and it is the lesser half. Locking away or even executing the one who did it does *nothing* for the one it was done to. A human court, at its absolute best, can only punish. It can never give back what was taken. The child is still gone. The wound is still real. If that is where the story ends, then justice is just a tidier kind of loss.

Here is where the God of the Bible does the thing no court on earth can do. The very life that was taken — that no human power could ever restore — He gives back. The Author of life is not stopped by death; He holds “the keys of hell and of death” (Revelation 1:18). What the criminal destroyed, God restores at the resurrection, and more than restores:

> “God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain: for the former things are passed away.”
> — Revelation 21:4

This is the part the scoreboard could never imagine. The victim's restoration *does not depend on the criminal making it right* — because he never could; you cannot hand back a life. It depends on God, who can. The grieving father of a dead child said the only thing that makes sense in the face of such loss: “I shall go to him, but he shall not return to me” (2 Samuel 12:23) — not *it is over,* but *I will see him again.* And of children in particular, Jesus said their angels “do always behold the face of My Father” (Matthew 18:10). The most defenseless are not beneath heaven's notice. They are nearest to its heart.

And the weight is not even close to even. “I reckon that the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed” (Romans 8:18). Whatever was taken will be answered not merely by repair, but by a restoration that swallows the loss whole.

## Why that is the fairest thing of all

Put the two halves together and you see a justice deeper than anything we have a name for. Our courts can do one thing: they can punish the guilty. They cannot restore the victim, and they cannot reach the guilty who slip away. God's justice does *both*, and lets no one slip. He collects every debt from the one who did it — saved or lost, fully, to the exact measure. And He restores every loss to the one it was done to — giving back even the life that no human hand could return. Neither side is cheated. The guilty escape nothing. The innocent lose nothing in the end.

That is the answer the question was really asking for. Not a God who looks away from a child's suffering, and not a God who lets monsters in clean while their victims stay broken — but a God exact enough to settle every account and powerful enough to undo every wound. He can be that fair for one reason: He did not stay outside of death and watch. He went into it Himself and came out holding its keys — which is how He can both bear the penalty and raise the dead. The One who will set it all right is not a distant accountant. He is the One who was killed by the cruelty of free men, and rose, and lives.

Nobody gets away. Nobody stays broken. That is not wishful thinking. It is the most exact fairness ever offered to a broken world — and it is the only one strong enough to look the suffering of a child full in the face without lying about it.

## The Witness of Scripture

The argument above rests on the convergence of many passages, gathered line upon line. Below is the fuller witness behind each movement — for anyone who wishes to weigh it.

### I. No one escapes — justice is real, exact, proportional, and beyond death

**Galatians 6:7** — whatever a man sows, he reaps. **Hebrews 9:27** — after death, the judgment. **Revelation 20:11–13** — the books opened; judged by works. **Luke 12:47–48** — many stripes or few, by what was known. **Romans 2:5–6** — rendered to every man according to his deeds. **2 Corinthians 5:10** — each gives account, good or bad. **Romans 12:19** — vengeance belongs to God, not the victim.

### II. The two debts — grace lifts the eternal penalty but not the reckoning for the deed

**2 Samuel 12:13–14** — David: sin put away (“thou shalt not die”), yet the consequence stands and the sword remains. **Colossians 3:25** — said to believers: he that does wrong receives for the wrong, no respect of persons. **1 Corinthians 3:13–15** — the saved are saved, yet the work is tried by fire and loss is suffered. **Hebrews 12:5–11** — the Father chastens the sons He receives. **Romans 6:23** — the wage of sin is death; eternal life is the gift.

### III. Even the worst can be reached — but are never thereby excused

**2 Chronicles 33:6, 12–13** — Manasseh, a shedder of innocent blood, found mercy when he turned. **1 Timothy 1:13–15** — Paul, a persecutor, called chief of sinners. **Romans 5:8** — Christ died for the ungodly — the gift offered, not justice for the deed waived.

### IV. The victim is fully restored — by the only One who can give back what was taken

**Revelation 1:18** — Christ holds the keys of death; He passed through it and lives. **Revelation 21:4** — every tear wiped away; death, sorrow, and pain ended. **Romans 8:18** — present suffering not worthy to be compared with the glory to come. **2 Samuel 12:23** — “I shall go to him”: the hope of reunion. **Matthew 18:10** — the angels of the little ones behold the Father's face. **1 Corinthians 15:42–44** — sown in corruption, raised in incorruption.
