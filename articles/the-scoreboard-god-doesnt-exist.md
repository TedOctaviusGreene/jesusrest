# The Scoreboard God Doesn't Exist

*She was right to ask. Why would heaven bless one person's bank account and ignore a child's cry? The honest answer is not the one religion usually gives — and it begins by admitting the question is fair.*

Some of the most thoughtful people who reject God reject Him for the strongest reason there is. They look at the world and see a child suffering something no child should ever know, with no rescue coming. Then they look again and see an ordinary person — no better than anyone — whose business does well, whose health holds, whose life seems quietly favored. And they ask the question every honest heart eventually asks: *If there is a God, why does He seem to bless the comfortable and abandon the helpless?*

Something here may surprise you, whichever side of belief you stand on: that question is right to ask. It is not an attack on faith; it is the question faith itself raises and refuses to dodge. The men and women who wrote the Bible asked it more sharply than any modern skeptic ever has. So before we answer it, let us honor it — because an answer that does not first feel the weight of the question is not worth hearing.

What follows is not my opinion. It is an attempt to let the Bible say, in its own words, what it actually claims about suffering, blessing, and justice. You may find it says something very different from what you were told it says.

## The hidden assumption

Underneath the question lies an assumption so common we rarely notice it: that life is a scoreboard. That prosperity is God's verdict of approval and suffering is His verdict of guilt. The comfortable man must have earned heaven's favor; the suffering child must have been, somehow, beyond its reach. Almost everyone — religious and not — carries this scoreboard in the back of their mind. And here is the strange thing: **the Bible attacks that assumption harder than the skeptic does.**

When a group of people once brought Jesus a report of worshippers slaughtered by a governor, and reminded Him of eighteen others crushed when a tower collapsed, they were doing exactly what the scoreboard demands — reading tragedy as a measure of guilt. Jesus answered flatly:

> “Suppose ye that these Galilaeans were sinners above all the Galilaeans, because they suffered such things? I tell you, Nay.”
> — Luke 13:2–3

He says *No* twice, once for each tragedy. The suffering did not measure their worth. And on the other side of the ledger, He is just as blunt about who receives the good things of this life:

> “He maketh His sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust.”
> — Matthew 5:45

Sun and rain — the ancient measure of provision — fall on everyone, the worthy and the unworthy alike. That is not a loophole in the system; it *is* the system. The comfortable man's comfort is, far more often than not, simple providence falling where it falls, not a medal pinned on his character. So the first half of the original question quietly dissolves. God is not *rewarding* the prosperous as righteous, and He is not *punishing* the suffering as guilty. The scoreboard was never there. We projected it.

One man in the Bible nearly lost his faith over this exact problem, and wrote down the whole crisis. His name was Asaph, and he was a worship leader — a believer. Read his confession and you will hear the skeptic's question in the mouth of a man who loved God:

> “But as for me, my feet were almost gone; my steps had well nigh slipped. For I was envious at the foolish, when I saw the prosperity of the wicked… They are not in trouble as other men… Verily I have cleansed my heart in vain.”
> — Psalm 73:2–3, 5, 13

He almost walked away. The prosperity of people who did not deserve it nearly undid him. And then one line turns the entire psalm:

> “Until I went into the sanctuary of God; then understood I their end.”
> — Psalm 73:17

The scoreboard reads only the present score. Asaph's despair broke the moment he saw that the game was not over — that there was an *end* still to come, where everything hidden would be set right. Hold that word. We will return to it, because it is the hinge on which the whole answer turns.

## Where the harm comes from

But the harder half of the question remains, and it is the half that should never be softened: the child. Not the comfortable man — the child. Where is the rule of protection? If God can act, and a child is being destroyed, why does heaven hold still?

Begin with what the Bible plainly refuses to say. It never says God authors the evil done to the innocent. It says the opposite:

> “Let no man say when he is tempted, I am tempted of God: for God cannot be tempted with evil, neither tempteth He any man.”
> — James 1:13

So where does the harm come from? The Bible names three sources, and God is not among them. There is a real adversary — “your adversary the devil, as a roaring lion, walketh about, seeking whom he may devour” (1 Peter 5:8). There is human will set free to choose, and able therefore to choose cruelty — the thief who “cometh not, but for to steal, and to kill, and to destroy” (John 10:10). And there is a whole creation knocked out of joint, “made subject to vanity,” groaning under a brokenness that touches everything in it (Romans 8:20–22).

Here is the part that is hard to accept but impossible to escape: love that cannot be refused is not love. A world in which free creatures genuinely choose is a world in which they can genuinely choose harm. God permitting a person to make a real choice is not the same as God making the choice for them. His restraint in one terrible moment is not His endorsement of what happens in it. The freedom that lets a mother love her child is the same freedom that, in another, becomes the capacity to wound. You cannot have the first without the possibility of the second. The skeptic feels this trade is too costly. The Bible agrees it is costly — and insists it is not the end of the story.

## “But where were the ones who should have protected them?”

There is an instinct, when we picture a hurt child, to look for the adults who were supposed to stand between that child and harm. The instinct is sound, and the Bible shares it. God does not leave the vulnerable un-championed. He delegates their care — to parents, to the strong, to anyone in a position to shield them — and He holds those stewards to a frightening account when they fail. Listen to how severely:

> “It were better for him that a millstone were hanged about his neck, and he cast into the sea, than that he should offend one of these little ones.”
> — Luke 17:2

That is not the language of a God who is indifferent to children. That is the language of a God who would rather a man drown than harm one. The shepherds of an earlier age who fed themselves and neglected the flock met His fury head-on: “Woe be to the shepherds… that do feed themselves! should not the shepherds feed the flocks?” (Ezekiel 34:2). Responsibility is real, and it runs uphill toward whoever held the power to protect.

But we must be careful here, because the scoreboard tries to sneak back in through this very door. It whispers that *every* suffering child must mean a guilty parent, that harm is always somebody's verdict. Jesus shut that door directly. When His own disciples looked at a man born blind and asked whose sin had caused it — his, or his parents' — He answered:

> “Neither hath this man sinned, nor his parents.”
> — John 9:3

So accountability is genuine, and it is severe, and it is also *not* a formula. Many who suffer were failed by those who should have stood guard, and those guardians will answer for it. But not every wound is a verdict on the wounded or on their family. The Bible holds both truths at once and refuses to collapse either one.

## Then why doesn't He act *now?*

This is the center of the ache, and it deserves a real answer rather than a comforting evasion. If God is just, and the wicked visibly prosper while the innocent visibly suffer, then either God is not just — or the books are not yet closed. The Bible's answer is the second, and it is emphatic: the settling of every account is real, it is exact, and much of it lies on the far side of death.

> “It is appointed unto men once to die, but after this the judgment.”
> — Hebrews 9:27

That word “judgment” is not the snap of a single sentence; it is the holding of a full reckoning, the way a judge presides over a matter until it is set right. And nothing is overlooked in it:

> “For we must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ; that every one may receive the things done in his body… whether it be good or bad.”
> — 2 Corinthians 5:10

And it is not a single flat measure dropped on everyone alike. The Bible is unusually specific that judgment is *proportional* — weighed exactly to what each person knew and did. Jesus said the servant who knew his master's will and defied it would be “beaten with many stripes,” while the one who did wrong in ignorance would be beaten “with few” (Luke 12:47–48). The instinct that a person who destroys a child should not receive the same measure as a petty fool is not human sentiment that the Bible has to correct. It *is* the Bible's own teaching. And that same exactness runs the other way too: this judgment is so fair that no one is ever condemned for a chance they never had — a fuller picture than we can open here, and one the Bible lays out in detail.

> The victim does not have to carry the scales. Someone stronger and more just has taken them up.

And to the one who was wronged — who cries out, as the martyrs in Revelation cry from beneath the altar, “How long… dost Thou not judge and avenge?” (Revelation 6:10) — the answer is not *never*. It is *not yet, but surely*. “Vengeance is Mine; I will repay, saith the Lord” (Romans 12:19). The reason justice often seems absent in the moment is not that it has been cancelled. It is that it has been moved to a courtroom where it cannot be bribed, escaped, or lost — the one Asaph glimpsed when he finally saw “their end.”

## The hardest mercy of all

There is one more thing the question contains, even when it is not spoken aloud, and it is the hardest. What about the person who did the harm? Christianity is known — sometimes resented — for claiming that even such a person can be forgiven. To a heart aching for a victim, that can sound like an insult. So let us be honest about exactly what the Bible does and does not say.

It does say the mercy reaches that far. “While we were yet sinners, Christ died for us” (Romans 5:8) — not for the respectable, but for the guilty. But it does *not* say the guilt simply evaporates, or that the victim's vindication is quietly cancelled. Forgiveness in the Bible is never the erasing of justice; it is the question of *who bears it.* When a notorious tax collector named Zacchaeus met grace, the proof that it was real was not a feeling — it was restitution: he gave back fourfold to everyone he had cheated (Luke 19:8). Genuine mercy received does not bypass the victim; it drives the forgiven person *toward* the victim to make it right.

And for the one who refuses even that — who will not turn, will not be remade, will not bear the cost or let Christ bear it — there is no third lane where they slip in lightly. The same courtroom that offers full pardon honors a final and real *No*. Grace is free. It is not cheap, and it is not forced.

## The answer that is not an explanation

Here is where the Bible does something no philosophy does. After everything — the dissolved scoreboard, the freedom that allows cruelty, the delegated care, the proportional justice held in reserve — its final answer to the suffering of the innocent is not a tidy explanation handed down from a safe distance. Its final answer is a Person who walked into the suffering Himself.

The God of the Bible did not stay outside the worst that human cruelty can do. He entered it. An innocent man, betrayed by those who should have protected Him, abandoned, tortured, and executed on a false charge — this is not the story of how God avoided suffering. It is the story of how God took the very worst of it into Himself. The prophets saw it coming centuries before: “He was wounded for our transgressions… and with His stripes we are healed” (Isaiah 53:5). So when you ask where God was while the innocent suffered, the Bible's strange and staggering reply is not a tidy excuse delivered from a safe distance. It is that God Himself walked into human suffering — and, having passed through death and out the other side, He holds the power to undo it.

And there is one line that holds this whole movement together — the line at the heart of everything we are about:

> “Christ in you, the hope of glory.”
> — Colossians 1:27

Notice the word *hope*. Glory is not yet held. It is promised, not possessed. Which means the groaning, the not-yet, the ache of a world still waiting to be set right — that is not a sign of God's absence. It is the shared condition of everyone still living between the cross and the day He makes all things new. He has not explained the wound out of existence. He has done something better: He has entered it, carried it, and sworn to undo it, when “God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying” (Revelation 21:4).

And the child — the child no one protected — is not lost in the gap. When a grieving king's infant son died, he said something the scoreboard could never say: “I shall go to him, but he shall not return to me” (2 Samuel 12:23) — the confidence of a reunion ahead. And of children Jesus said, “their angels do always behold the face of My Father which is in heaven” (Matthew 18:10). The most defenseless are not beneath heaven's notice. They are nearest to its heart.

## To the one still asking

If you do not believe, this is not a request to abandon your hardest question. It is an invitation to notice that the Bible asked it first, and more fiercely than any skeptic has — and that it never answered with a scoreboard. It answered with a God who refuses to treat your suffering as a verdict, who holds a real and exact justice in reserve where no victim's cry is finally ignored, and who entered the worst of it personally rather than narrate it from the clouds.

If you do believe, then lay down the scoreboard for good. Stop reading your comfort as a medal or someone else's pain as a sentence. The same providence that warms your day warms your enemy's. The accounts you cannot balance are not unbalanced; they are simply not yet closed. And the One who will close them is the same One who was nailed to a cross by the cruelty of free men, and rose, and now lives — not at a distance, but, if you will have Him, within you. That is the only hope strong enough to look the suffering of a child full in the face and not look away: not an explanation, but a Person, and a promise that He is not finished yet.

The scoreboard god does not exist. He never did. What exists is something the scoreboard could never imagine — a God who would rather bear the wound than keep His distance from it.

## The Witness of Scripture

The argument above rests on the convergence of many passages, gathered line upon line. Below is the fuller witness behind each movement — for anyone who wishes to weigh it for themselves.

### I. There is no scoreboard — suffering and prosperity are not verdicts

**Luke 13:1–5** — Jesus denies that the slain and the crushed were sinners above others. **Matthew 5:45** — sun and rain fall on the evil and the good alike. **John 9:1–3** — the man born blind sinned no more than anyone. **Job 42:7** — God's anger against the friends who read Job's suffering as guilt. **Ecclesiastes 9:11** — time and chance happen to all. **Psalm 73 (whole)** — Asaph's crisis over the prosperity of the wicked, resolved in the sanctuary at the sight of “their end” (vv. 17–19).

### II. The harm comes from free will, the adversary, and a fallen creation — not from God

**James 1:13** — God tempts no man with evil. **1 Peter 5:8** — the adversary prowls like a lion. **John 10:10** — the thief comes to steal, kill, destroy. **Romans 8:20–22** — creation subjected to vanity, groaning. **Genesis 3** — the entrance of the choice, and of death. **Deuteronomy 30:19** — “choose life”: real freedom, real consequence.

### III. God delegates the care of the vulnerable and holds the strong accountable

**Luke 17:2** — the millstone warning over harming a little one. **Ezekiel 34:1–10** — woe to shepherds who feed themselves and neglect the flock. **Ephesians 6:4** — parents charged with their children's nurture. **Psalm 82:3–4** — defend the poor and fatherless. **James 1:27** — pure religion visits the orphan in affliction. **John 9:3** — yet suffering is not always a verdict on the sufferer or the family.

### IV. Justice is real, exact, proportional, and largely beyond this life

**Hebrews 9:27** — after death, the judgment. **2 Corinthians 5:10** — each receives for the things done, good or bad. **Galatians 6:7** — whatever a man sows he reaps. **Luke 12:47–48** — many stripes or few, by what was known. **Romans 2:5–6** — rendered to every man according to his deeds. **Revelation 6:9–11** — the martyrs' cry, and the “not yet, but surely.” **Revelation 20:11–13** — the books opened, judged by works. **Romans 12:19** — vengeance belongs to God, not the victim.

### V. Mercy reaches the guilty — without cancelling the victim's vindication

**Romans 5:6–8** — Christ died for the ungodly. **Luke 19:8–9** — Zacchaeus: real grace produces real restitution. **2 Chronicles 33:10–13** — even Manasseh, a shedder of innocent blood, found mercy when he turned. **1 Timothy 1:15** — Paul, a persecutor, called chief of sinners. **John 3:18, 36** — the real and final “No” for the one who refuses.

### VI. The final answer is a Person who entered the suffering

**Isaiah 53:3–5** — wounded for our transgressions; by His stripes we are healed. **Colossians 1:27** — Christ in you, the hope (not yet the possession) of glory. **Hebrews 4:15** — a High Priest touched with our infirmities. **2 Samuel 12:23** — “I shall go to him”: the hope of reunion. **Matthew 18:10** — the angels of the little ones behold the Father's face. **Revelation 21:4** — every tear wiped away; death and sorrow ended.
