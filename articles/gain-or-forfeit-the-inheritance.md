# You Can Gain or Forfeit the Inheritance and Still Be Saved

*The system keeps two separate accounts — salvation, which is secure, and reward, which a son or daughter of God can gain or lose.*

**The Salvation System — Part 5 of Eight**

This article is about one part of a single, connected system of salvation, and it answers a question that confuses almost everyone: if salvation cannot be earned or lost, then what exactly is being gained or lost when the Bible warns believers and promises them rewards? Here is the whole system in one sentence, so that this part can be seen in its place: God's salvation is a gift that a person cannot earn and cannot lose; the person who receives that gift becomes a son or a daughter of God; a son or daughter who lives by the flesh is disciplined but never disowned; a son or daughter who yields to Christ bears Christ's own fruit and is rewarded for it; and at no point in the entire system can anyone boast.

The system keeps two separate accounts, and almost every confusion about salvation comes from collapsing the two accounts into one. The first account is salvation itself — the gift, received by faith, which a son or daughter of God can neither earn nor lose. The second account is reward, also called the inheritance — what a son or daughter of God gains or forfeits, depending on whether that son or daughter yielded to Christ. This article shows that the two accounts are genuinely separate, so that a son or daughter of God can lose the reward entirely and still remain fully saved.

## The clearest verse in the Bible on this

Paul draws the line in plain daylight:

> Now if any man build upon this foundation gold, silver, precious stones, wood, hay, stubble; every man's work shall be made manifest… the fire shall try every man's work of what sort it is. If any man's work abide which he hath built thereupon, he shall receive a reward. If any man's work shall be burned, he shall suffer loss: but he himself shall be saved; yet so as by fire.
>
> — 1 Corinthians 3:12–15 KJV

Read it slowly. One person's life-work survives the fire, and that person receives a reward. Another person's life-work is burned to nothing, and that person suffers loss — and then, in the same breath, "*but he himself shall be saved.*" There it is, settled in a single verse: a person can lose everything that person built and still be saved. The reward can burn while the person is pulled from the flames. Salvation and reward are two different accounts.

That is why every believer must still appear before the judgment seat — not to find out whether the believer is saved, but to receive what the believer actually did:

> For we must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ; that every one may receive the things done in his body, according to that he hath done, whether it be good or bad.
>
> — 2 Corinthians 5:10 KJV

> Knowing that of the Lord ye shall receive the reward of the inheritance: for ye serve the Lord Christ. But he that doeth wrong shall receive for the wrong which he hath done.
>
> — Colossians 3:24–25 KJV

Grace forgives the eternal penalty completely; Christ alone could carry that penalty, and He did. But grace does not erase the earthly consequences of a wasted or wounding life. A son or daughter of God is fully forgiven and still reaps what that son or daughter has sown. Both are true, and the system needs both.

## "Inherit the kingdom" is not "have eternal life"

This same distinction is the key to a set of verses that have frightened sincere believers for generations — the warnings that the unrighteous "shall not inherit the kingdom":

> Know ye not that the unrighteous shall not inherit the kingdom of God?… And such were some of you: but ye are washed, but ye are sanctified, but ye are justified in the name of the Lord Jesus, and by the Spirit of our God.
>
> — 1 Corinthians 6:9, 11 KJV

Watch what Paul actually does. Paul warns about *inheriting the kingdom* — and then immediately tells these same believers that they are already washed, already sanctified, already justified. Paul does not say, "so you might not be saved." Paul says, "such *were* some of you — but you are not that anymore." The warning is real, but the warning is about the inheritance, not about the gift of life, which is already secure.

This matters because "inheriting the kingdom" and "having eternal life" are not the same phrase. To *have* eternal life is a present possession, received by faith, that a son or daughter of God can actually know they hold:

> And this is the record, that God hath given to us eternal life, and this life is in His Son… These things have I written unto you that believe on the name of the Son of God; that ye may know that ye have eternal life.
>
> — 1 John 5:11, 13 KJV

To *inherit* the kingdom is the language of an heir — a portion handed down, a place to reign, a reward that depends on whether a son or daughter let Christ rule in them now. That is why Scripture ties the inheritance directly to suffering with Christ and reigning with Christ:

> And if children, then heirs; heirs of God, and joint-heirs with Christ; if so be that we suffer with Him, that we may be also glorified together.
>
> — Romans 8:17 KJV

> If we suffer, we shall also reign with Him: if we deny Him, He also will deny us.
>
> — 2 Timothy 2:12 KJV

Notice the small word that the gift never carries: *if*. Eternal life is never offered with an "if"; eternal life is given. But reigning with Christ — the crown, the throne, the rule — is held out with an "if," because reigning belongs to the reward account, not the gift account. Paul lived his whole life with that reward in view, and he feared, not the loss of his salvation, but disqualification from the prize:

> I keep under my body, and bring it into subjection: lest that by any means, when I have preached to others, I myself should be a castaway.
>
> — 1 Corinthians 9:27 KJV

The word Paul used was the language of the athletic games: a runner disqualified from the prize, not a person thrown out of the family. Paul was running for a crown, and Paul knew the crown could be missed.

## What the reward actually is

The reward itself is staggering. The reward is to reign with Christ in His kingdom — the thousand-year reign promised to those who overcome:

> To him that overcometh will I grant to sit with Me in My throne, even as I also overcame, and am set down with My Father in His throne.
>
> — Revelation 3:21 KJV

> And they lived and reigned with Christ a thousand years… they shall be priests of God and of Christ, and shall reign with Him a thousand years.
>
> — Revelation 20:4, 6 KJV

A throne. A crown (Revelation 2:10). A new name (Revelation 2:17). Authority over the nations (Revelation 2:26–27). None of these is handed out for merely being saved. Each one is the reward of a son or daughter of God who let Christ reign in them now, so that Christ could reign through them then. So a saved person who spends a whole life on self-will — even a person sincerely trying to be a better human being — may stand among the saved and still not inherit that reign. That person is in the family forever. That person simply did not lay hold of the crown.

> **Saved is the gift. Reigning is the reward. Confuse the two, and the whole Bible seems to break.**

## Why the two accounts must stay separate

So the system keeps two accounts, and it keeps them separate. Salvation is the gift, and it cannot be lost. Reward is the inheritance, and it can be gained or forfeited by a son or daughter of God while salvation itself stands untouched. Holding these two accounts apart is what allows every warning in the Bible to keep its full force without ever threatening the gift.

It is also the key that unlocks the passage people most often use against this whole system — the words of James about faith and works. James is the subject of the next article.
