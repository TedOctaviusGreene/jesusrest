# He Didn’t Seem to Show Up

*What’s really happening when you yield to Christ and feel left alone with yourself*

You did the thing. You meant it.

Somewhere quiet and real, you decided you were done running yourself. You said it the way a person says it only when they’ve finally run out of other options: *not my will. Live through me, because I clearly cannot do this on my own.* You meant to die to yourself. You meant to abide, to yield, to step aside and let Another live.

And then an ordinary moment came. The sharp word you didn’t want to say. The old pride rising on schedule. The same anger, the same appetite, the same selfishness you were sure you had handed over the night before. And you said it, did it, were it — again — as though nothing had happened. As though no one had come.

The failure stings. But underneath it is a question that stings worse, and quieter: *Where were You? I asked. I waited. I yielded. And You didn’t show up.*

If you have ever stood in that exact place, this is written for you. Not to talk you out of what you felt — what you felt was real — but to tell you what was actually happening, because it is not what it looked like.

## Let’s make the despair honest first

It would be cheap to skip past how reasonable the dark conclusion feels. So let’s say it plainly, in its strongest form.

Maybe surrender is a beautiful idea that simply breaks against a real Tuesday. Maybe the people who talk about Christ living through them got something you didn’t get. Maybe you asked and were met with silence because you are not really His — or because it works for others and not for you. Maybe the honest thing is to admit it doesn’t work and stop pretending.

Anyone who has tried in earnest — really tried, not as a slogan but as a last resort — has stood at the edge of that thought. It is not a foolish thought. It deserves a real answer.

Here is the answer, and it is specific: He did not fail to show up. Something else happened — and Scripture describes it so exactly that there is an entire chapter devoted to the very feeling you just had.

## The most honest chapter about exactly this

In the seventh chapter of Romans, a man describes your night.

> For that which I do I allow not: for what I would, that do I not; but what I hate, that do I.
> — Romans 7:15

> For the good that I would I do not: but the evil which I would not, that I do.
> — Romans 7:19

And then the cry that is probably closer to what you actually felt than any prayer you managed:

> O wretched man that I am! who shall deliver me?
> — Romans 7:24

This is not a passage about a faithless person. It is the most thorough description in all of Scripture of a genuine person who wants the good, hates the evil, and does the evil anyway. It is your experience, written down two thousand years before you lived it.

Now notice something that changes everything once you see it. Read that chapter and count how often one small word appears: *I.* I do. I would. I hate. I find. I see. It is there dozens of times in a few short verses. And in all of that straining, willing, and failing, there is almost no mention of the Spirit at all — not until the chapter turns.

That is the whole diagnosis in a single observation. Romans 7 is the sound of a person trying to perform righteousness by the strength of his own will. And the harder that “I” tries, the more total the defeat. The defeat is not evidence that help was withheld. The defeat is what self-effort produces, every time, no matter how sincere.

Then the chapter breaks open:

> The law of the Spirit of life in Christ Jesus hath made me free from the law of sin and death.
> — Romans 8:2

The deliverance was never going to come from a better, more determined version of the same straining. It comes from a different power entirely.

## The trap hiding inside “yielding”

Here is where it gets subtle, and where most sincere people get caught without ever noticing.

There is a question put to people who began their walk by simple trust:

> Are ye so foolish? having begun in the Spirit, are ye now made perfect by the flesh?
> — Galatians 3:3

Read that slowly, because it names the exact trap. A person can begin in the Spirit — receive Christ by faith, freely, no striving — and then quietly try to finish the job by flesh-effort. And the cruelest version of this is when the flesh-effort disguises itself as surrender.

Watch how it happens. “Abide in Christ” becomes a thing you perform. “Yield” becomes something you do harder. “Die to self” turns into gritting your teeth and trying, by raw willpower, to not be angry, to not be proud, to not want what you want. And the moment yielding becomes another act of your own strength, you have walked straight back into Romans 7 — only now you’re doing it in spiritual language, which makes the eventual collapse feel even more like God’s failure instead of your own misplaced effort.

> You weren’t wrong to want to yield. You were wrong about who was supposed to do the work once you did.

## The other trap: waiting that isn’t trust

There is a second way this goes wrong, and it is gentler and harder to catch, because it looks like obedience.

Some people, having heard that striving is the problem, do the opposite. They stop. They wait. They go quiet and still and hold their breath for Christ to “show up” — to take over, to produce the feeling, to visibly move so they can finally relax. This looks like rest. It looks like faith. It is neither.

There was a generation that did exactly this. They had stopped striving — they were waiting in a wilderness — and yet Scripture says the promise of rest

> did not profit them, not being mixed with faith in them that heard it.
> — Hebrews 4:2

That phrase is the key to the whole thing. *Not mixed with faith.* They had ceased their own labor, but they had not replaced it with belief. They had replaced it with waiting and watching to see if it would work — which is not trust at all. It is the opposite of trust. Trust does not stand at the door checking whether the One inside is really there. Testing and trusting are contradictions.

That is what so many sincere people are actually doing in the moment they call surrender. They are not gripping anymore — good. But they are not believing either. They are conducting surveillance. They are waiting on a Christ they have not yet trusted is already present, watching to see if He will prove Himself before they will rest in Him. And so it feels like silence, when in truth they were waiting in the same room as the One they were waiting for.

## You already have what you were waiting to receive

Here is the ground the whole thing has to stand on, and it is finished ground.

If you are in Christ, His righteousness is not a reward you are inching toward. It is already, entirely, yours.

> But of Him are ye in Christ Jesus, who of God is made unto us… righteousness.
> — 1 Corinthians 1:30

> For He hath made Him to be sin for us, who knew no sin; that we might be made the righteousness of God in Him.
> — 2 Corinthians 5:21

Sit with what that means for the night you failed. You were straining toward a standing you already possessed. You were waiting to receive a Person who had already moved in. The whole frantic question — *do I have this yet, is it working, will He come* — was built on something already settled before you asked it.

> It is finished.
> — John 19:30

This is why the answer is not “try to surrender better next time.” The work of becoming righteous is not a work you can do better. It is a work that is done. There is nothing left to achieve there — only something to believe. And the person who truly believes it stops straining, not because they gave up, but because they finally saw there was nothing left to earn.

And out of that resting belief, the fruit you were trying to manufacture actually begins to grow — not because you squeezed harder, but because a branch that stops straining and simply stays joined to the vine is the one that bears.

> I am the vine, ye are the branches: He that abideth in me, and I in him, the same bringeth forth much fruit: for without me ye can do nothing.
> — John 15:5

## But don’t turn belief into a new kind of straining

This is the moment to set a guard, because there is a version of all this that becomes its own quiet prison.

It is possible to take “just believe” and turn believing itself into a work — to start straining to believe hard enough, to crank your faith up to some intensity you imagine will finally trigger the result, and then, when you still fail, to conclude that your struggle proves you didn’t believe strongly enough. That is Romans 7 again, wearing a new coat. Now instead of checking your behavior you’re checking your faith, and the treadmill turns just the same.

Scripture cuts that idea off at the root. The measure that matters was never the intensity of your belief.

> If ye had faith as a grain of mustard seed…
> — Luke 17:6

The smallest seed there is — and it is enough. And the man whose child was healed did not arrive with towering certainty; he arrived honest:

> Lord, I believe; help thou mine unbelief.
> — Mark 9:24

Weak faith, admitted faith, mixed faith — and it was met. It was never the size of the believing. It was the One believed in. Tiny faith in a great Christ is not a small thing, because the power is His and not in the strength of your gripping it. So if you are reading this still feeling that your faith is too small to count — that is exactly the size faith is allowed to be. Bring it as it is.

## The battle is real — even for the strongest believer

One more thing has to be said honestly, or this whole thing becomes a fresh weight on your back. It would be easy to walk away thinking: *so the struggle only happens to people who don’t really believe — once I believe enough, the war will simply end.* That is not true, and it is important that it is not true, because believing it will crush you the next time you stumble.

The man who wrote about being free from the law of sin and death also wrote this about himself, late in his life, at the height of his faith:

> Not as though I had already attained, either were already perfect… I press toward the mark.
> — Philippians 3:12–14

> I keep under my body, and bring it into subjection.
> — 1 Corinthians 9:27

That is not a doubting man reporting a faith-failure. That is the strongest of believers describing pressing and discipline as his ordinary, lifelong condition. The struggle between what you want and what you do is not proof you failed to believe. It is the standing condition of every person who still lives in a body not yet redeemed.

> The flesh lusteth against the Spirit, and the Spirit against the flesh… so that ye cannot do the things that ye would.
> — Galatians 5:17

That war is written, plainly, as the present terms of the walk — for everyone. So the war does not end when you believe. Here is what changes: you stop fighting it from defeat and begin standing in it from victory. The work of becoming righteous is finished — that is over; believe it and rest. The walk remains, and it remains for everyone, even for the greatest of the saints. And faith is not what ends the walk. Faith is what lets Christ win it through you instead of you losing it on your own.

## So what really happened the night He “didn’t show up”

Put it all together, and the silence you heard was never His absence.

He did not fail to come. One of two things happened, and likely both. Either you quietly took the controls back and called your own straining “surrender” — and the harder you strained, the more certainly you failed, exactly as that seventh chapter promises. Or you stopped straining but never started trusting — you waited and watched and tested, conducting surveillance on a Christ you had not yet believed was already in the room, and called that unbelief by the name of rest.

And under all of it, you were reaching to receive a righteousness you already fully possessed, and waiting on a Presence that had already taken up residence in you the moment you became His.

None of this requires you to be without sin now. There is no flawless version of you waiting on the far side of enough faith. *“If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves”* (1 John 1:8). The walk has stumbling built into it. And for the stumbling there is this, held in the very same breath as the call to holiness:

> My little children, these things write I unto you, that ye sin not. And if any man sin, we have an advocate with the Father, Jesus Christ the righteous.
> — 1 John 2:1

So here is where it lands, and you can rest the whole weight of yourself on it. The work of becoming righteous is finished — believe it, and rest. The walk remains, for everyone, even for the strongest who ever lived. And faith does not end that walk. Faith is simply trusting the One who is already there — and letting Him live the life that is already yours.

> *He was never the One who didn’t show up.*  
> *He was already in the room. You were waiting on Someone*  
> *you were standing beside the whole time.*
