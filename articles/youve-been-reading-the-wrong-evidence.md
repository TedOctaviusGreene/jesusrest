# You've Been Reading the Wrong Evidence

*On the mistake of trying to tell whether God loves you by looking at your life — and the proof that was settled long before you asked.*

There is a moment in an old story that almost everyone recognizes the shape of, even if they have never read the book.

A woman has decided what to think of a certain man. She finds him proud, cold, beneath her good opinion. She has the evidence, or thinks she does — his manner, his reputation, the way things looked. And then her family is struck by a disaster: a younger sister runs off and disgraces them all, the kind of scandal that, in that world, could ruin every woman in the household for life. It is hopeless. And then, quietly, without anyone knowing, the man she had written off goes and fixes it. He hunts down the sister. He pays off the man who ruined her. He arranges the marriage that saves the family's name. He bears the embarrassment and the cost himself, and he does it all in secret, wanting no thanks, expecting nothing in return — and he does it for a family that had wronged him, while she still thought the worst of him.

When she finds out and tries to thank him, he will not let her frame it as a favor to her family. In Jane Austen's words:

> If you will thank me, let it be for yourself alone… But your family owe me nothing. Much as I respect them, I believe I thought only of you.
> — Jane Austen, *Pride and Prejudice*

She had been reading the wrong evidence the whole time. She thought she was looking at a man who didn't care, when in fact she was loved — and the proof was already finished, already paid for, already done, before she felt a single thing in return. The love had never been the question. Her ability to read it had.

That is the exact mistake almost every person makes about God. And it is the mistake this is written to undo.

## The question underneath

Here is the question, said plainly, because most people carry it without ever putting it into words: *Does God love me?* And the way we try to answer it is by looking at our lives. We scan our circumstances for the verdict. When things go well, we quietly feel approved of. When things go badly — the illness, the lost job, the prayer that went unanswered, the season where nothing seems blessed — a cold thought slips in: *maybe He's displeased with me. Maybe I've done something. Maybe if I were more obedient, more faithful, more disciplined, the blessings would come and the troubles would lift, and then I'd know He was pleased.*

Notice what has happened in that line of thinking. We have turned the relationship into a transaction, and our circumstances into a scoreboard. And the moment we do that, we have walked straight into the oldest religious error there is: the belief that we must perform in order to be loved, and that our lives will display the running total. It feels like faith. It is the opposite. And the whole of Scripture was written, in part, to pull us out of it.

Because the verdict on whether God loves you is not being calculated daily from your circumstances. It was rendered once, finally, in the past — and it is the most settled fact in the universe.

## The proof is behind you, not around you

This is the heart of it, so let it land. The proof of God's love is not something you find by looking at your present situation. It is something you find by looking back at what He already did, before you could have earned it and while you were at your absolute worst.

> But God commendeth His love toward us, in that, while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us.
> — Romans 5:8

Read every word of the timing. Not "after we cleaned ourselves up." Not "once we proved we were worth it." *While we were yet sinners* — at our worst, having done nothing, deserving nothing, returning nothing — that is when the love was proven, in the most costly act imaginable. The demonstration is finished. It sits in the past, permanent, like the secret rescue the woman only discovered later.

> Herein is love, not that we loved God, but that He loved us, and sent His Son to be the propitiation for our sins.
> — 1 John 4:10

He thought only of you. Before you loved Him back. Before you could have. The sacrifice was already made. So when you scan your present circumstances trying to figure out whether God loves you, you are doing exactly what the woman in the story did — staring at the wrong evidence, missing the thing already done. You are asking a question that was answered, at unimaginable cost, before you were born.

## But doesn't the Bible say obey and be blessed?

Now we have to be honest and give the strongest version of the other side, because it is not made up. There really is a place in Scripture where obedience is tied directly to blessing and disobedience directly to disaster — and it is stated with overwhelming force.

It is found in the covenant God made with the nation of Israel at Sinai. Read how absolute it is:

> And it shall come to pass, if thou shalt hearken diligently unto the voice of the Lord thy God… that the Lord thy God will set thee on high above all nations of the earth… Blessed shalt thou be in the city, and blessed shalt thou be in the field. Blessed shall be the fruit of thy body, and the fruit of thy ground.
> — Deuteronomy 28:1–4

And then the other side, just as absolute:

> But it shall come to pass, if thou wilt not hearken unto the voice of the Lord thy God… that all these curses shall come upon thee… Cursed shalt thou be in the city, and cursed shalt thou be in the field.
> — Deuteronomy 28:15–16

There it is, in black and white: obey and be blessed in body, field, and basket; disobey and be cursed in the same. If that is how God deals with His people, then reading your circumstances for a verdict is not foolish at all — it is simply checking the contract. So the question becomes unavoidable and sharp: is this the arrangement you are living under? Does God still run your life on this engine — prosperity for obedience, hardship for disobedience? We will not dodge it. We will let Scripture answer, and the answer is remarkably clear once you gather all of it.

## Let the Bible answer

Watch what Jesus Himself does to the idea that good circumstances track with God's approval and bad ones with His displeasure. He breaks the link directly:

> That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven: for He maketh His sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust.
> — Matthew 5:45

Sun and rain — the very pictures of blessing — fall on the wicked and the righteous alike. You cannot read a person's standing with God off of them.

Then He goes further and forbids, outright, the reading of calamity as a verdict of guilt. People brought Him a report of some who had died in a horror, assuming such a fate meant they were especially sinful, and He stopped that thinking cold:

> Or those eighteen, upon whom the tower in Siloam fell, and slew them, think ye that they were sinners above all men that dwelt in Jerusalem? I tell you, Nay.
> — Luke 13:4–5

When His own disciples saw a man born blind and assumed the suffering proved someone had sinned, He rejected the premise entirely: *"Neither hath this man sinned, nor his parents: but that the works of God should be made manifest in him"* (John 9:3).

And it is not only Jesus. The honest writers of the Old Testament wrestled with this exact thing and came down hard against the circumstance-reading. One of them nearly lost his faith staring at how well the wicked were doing and how much the faithful suffered — until something shifted:

> When I thought to know this, it was too painful for me; until I went into the sanctuary of God; then understood I their end.
> — Psalm 73:16–17

Notice the resolution: not "then I saw the wicked finally suffer in this life," but "I understood their *end*." The accounts are not settled here. They are settled later. And another writer states the present-life reality without flinching:

> There is a just man that perisheth in his righteousness, and there is a wicked man that prolongeth his life in his wickedness.
> — Ecclesiastes 7:15

The righteous sometimes die early; the wicked sometimes live long and easy. That is simply the observed truth of this age, and the Bible says it plainly rather than pretending otherwise.

And then the heaviest witness of all. There is a famous chapter listing the great heroes of faith, and it splits them into two groups that should end this question forever. Some, through faith, *"subdued kingdoms, wrought righteousness, obtained promises, stopped the mouths of lions."* But the very same chapter, of people with the very same faith, continues:

> Others were tortured, not accepting deliverance… and others had trial of cruel mockings and scourgings, yea, moreover of bonds and imprisonment: they were stoned, they were sawn asunder… they wandered about… being destitute, afflicted, tormented.
> — Hebrews 11:35–37

Same faith. Opposite circumstances. Some conquered; some were sawn in two. And then the verdict on all of them together, conquerors and martyrs alike:

> And these all, having obtained a good report through faith, received not the promise.
> — Hebrews 11:39

Every one of them — faithful, commended, loved — and not one received the promise in this life. If circumstances were the measure of God's pleasure, this chapter is impossible. It exists precisely to tell you they are not.

And here is the detail that should bury the idea completely. If anything, faithfulness in this age correlates with more hardship, not less:

> Yea, and all that will live godly in Christ Jesus shall suffer persecution.
> — 2 Timothy 3:12

The most obedient man who ever lived suffered the most of all. So the equation "obedience equals an easier, more prosperous life" is not merely unproven by Scripture — it is reversed by it.

## So what was Sinai, then?

If the obey-and-prosper, disobey-and-suffer arrangement is so clearly not how God runs the believer's life, what was it? Scripture is direct: it was a specific covenant, made with a specific nation, at a specific time, with terms that were national and earthly by design. It governed Israel's life in their land — their harvests, their cities, their enemies, their rain. It was real, and it was theirs.

But it was never the permanent or universal way God relates to His people, and Scripture itself says a new and better arrangement has come:

> In that He saith, A new covenant, He hath made the first old. Now that which decayeth and waxeth old is ready to vanish away.
> — Hebrews 8:13

The believer in Christ is not living under the Sinai contract, scanning the fields for blessings and curses. That is not the framework anymore. Which means the entire project of reading your bank account, your health, or your hardships as a report card on God's pleasure is built on a covenant that is not yours.

## What about the curse — does God still curse disobedience?

This is the fear under the fear, so let us answer it cleanly: does God curse the disobedient believer? No. And the reason is not that the curse was waved off as if it never mattered. The reason is that it was borne.

> Christ hath redeemed us from the curse of the law, being made a curse for us: for it is written, Cursed is every one that hangeth on a tree.
> — Galatians 3:13

The curse did not evaporate. It landed — on Him. He absorbed it so that it would never land on you. Which is why the verdict over the person in Christ is not "cursed when disobedient, blessed when obedient," but something far more stable:

> There is therefore now no condemnation to them which are in Christ Jesus.
> — Romans 8:1

So what remains when a believer goes wrong? Two things, and neither is the covenant-curse. First, the loving discipline of a Father correcting a child He will never disown — which Scripture insists is the opposite of condemnation, the proof of sonship rather than its loss. Second, the ordinary reaping of what is sown: *"Be not deceived; God is not mocked: for whatsoever a man soweth, that shall he also reap"* (Galatians 6:7). Actions have wakes; choices have consequences. But consequence is not curse, and discipline is not rejection. Both happen inside a love that is fixed and a standing that cannot be lost.

## The promise of prosperity — what Scripture does to it

There is a popular message, in many forms, that takes the blessing half of the Sinai covenant and applies it directly to the believer today: have enough faith, give enough, obey enough, and God is obligated to make you healthy and prosperous now. It sounds biblical because it is quoting real verses — it is simply quoting the wrong covenant, aimed at the wrong people, in the wrong age.

Let Scripture itself dismantle it. If material prosperity were the reward of faith, then the apostles, with the greatest faith in the record, should have been the wealthiest and most comfortable men alive. Instead, one of them wrote this about his own life:

> Even unto this present hour we both hunger, and thirst, and are naked, and are buffeted, and have no certain dwellingplace… being defamed, we intreat: we are made as the filth of the world, and are the offscouring of all things unto this day.
> — 1 Corinthians 4:11–13

And he warned, in plain words, against the very people who treat godliness as a strategy for getting rich:

> Perverse disputings of men of corrupt minds, and destitute of the truth, supposing that gain is godliness: from such withdraw thyself.
> — 1 Timothy 6:5

To suppose *"that gain is godliness"* — to read wealth as the marker of God's favor — is named here as the mark of a corrupted mind, not a faithful one. The true contentment Scripture commends is the opposite of the prosperity equation: *"But godliness with contentment is great gain"* (1 Timothy 6:6). The gain is the contentment itself, not the payout.

## But doesn't obedience still matter? — yes, and here is how

It would be a serious mistake to swing to the opposite error and conclude that, since circumstances don't measure God's love, obedience therefore does nothing. It does. It simply does something other than buying His love or stocking your life with material rewards. There are two real things it does, and both are precious.

First, there is the present fruit of a yielded life — not a payment negotiated with God, but the natural overflow of His life flowing through you when you abide in Him. *"But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, longsuffering, gentleness, goodness, faith, meekness, temperance"* (Galatians 5:22–23). *"Great peace have they which love thy law: and nothing shall offend them"* (Psalm 119:165). This is real blessing, available now — but notice it is inward and spiritual, the character and peace of Christ formed in you, not a guarantee of ease in your outward circumstances.

Second, there is future reward — a real recompense for the fruit Christ bears through a yielded life, assessed not by today's circumstances but at the end:

> Every man's work shall be made manifest… If any man's work abide… he shall receive a reward. If any man's work shall be burned, he shall suffer loss: but he himself shall be saved.
> — 1 Corinthians 3:13–15

Read the last line carefully, because it guards everything: reward can be lost, but the person is saved regardless. Which means the reward is never the same thing as the love or the salvation. The love is fixed and free. The reward is the harvest of how you responded to it.

So yes — God rewards obedience. But the reward is present spiritual fruit and future recompense, not a this-life scoreboard of money and ease. And none of it is the price of His love. All of it flows from a love already given.

## So what is the "obedience" He actually wants?

Here is where it all resolves, and it is the simplest part. If we are not under the Sinai contract of rules-for-rewards, what is the one thing left to obey?

It is not a checklist. It is a Person. The single "obedience" of this new arrangement is to yield to Christ and walk in the Spirit — which is to say, to stop running your own life and let Him live His through you. *"This is His commandment, That we should believe on the name of His Son Jesus Christ"* (1 John 3:23). *"If we live in the Spirit, let us also walk in the Spirit"* (Galatians 5:25). The obedience He wants is not your performance offered to earn His favor. It is your surrender, opening the door so the One who already loves you can live the life only He can live.

> **The love came first. Your obedience does not generate it — it answers it.**

And that yielding is always a response, never a purchase. This is the line that holds the whole thing together: *"We love Him, because He first loved us"* (1 John 4:19). It was proven first, in the past, at the cross, while you were at your worst. Just as the woman in the story did not earn the rescue by finally loving the man back — she discovered a love already proven, and loved him because of what she found had been done.

## Where this lands

You have been reading the wrong evidence. The illness, the setback, the unblessed season, the prayer that seemed to go unanswered — none of that is the verdict on whether God loves you, any more than the woman's first cold impressions were the truth about the man who had quietly saved her family. The verdict is not being tallied from your circumstances day by day. It was rendered once, in the past, in blood, before you did a single thing to deserve it.

So you can stop. Stop scanning your life for proof of a love that was already proven. Stop trying to obey your way into being loved, and stop reading your hardships as evidence you've fallen out of favor. The Sinai engine of blessing-for-obedience and curse-for-disobedience is not the arrangement you live under: the curse was carried by Christ, the love cannot be earned or lost, and your circumstances were never the measure of either.

What is left is not a contract to keep but a love to answer.

> *He thought only of you.*
> *He did it before you knew, before you cared, before you could give anything back.*
> *The proof is finished, and it is behind you — permanent, and paid for.*
> *The only fitting response is not to earn it, but to believe it,*
> *and to open your life and let the One who already loves you live.*
