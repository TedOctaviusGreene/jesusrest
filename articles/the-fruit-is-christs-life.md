# The Fruit Is Christ's Life, Not Your Effort

*How a son or daughter of God actually lives a life that pleases God — by yielding to Christ, not by trying harder.*

**The Salvation System — Part 4 of Eight**

This article is about one part of a single, connected system of salvation, and it answers the question the last part left open: how does a son or daughter of God live a life that actually pleases God? Here is the whole system in one sentence, so that this part can be seen in its place: God's salvation is a gift that a person cannot earn and cannot lose; the person who receives that gift becomes a son or a daughter of God; a son or daughter who lives by the flesh is disciplined but never disowned; a son or daughter who yields to Christ bears Christ's own fruit and is rewarded for it; and at no point in the entire system can anyone boast.

The system has shown that a son or daughter of God who lives by the flesh is disciplined. That raises an obvious question: how does a son or daughter of God live any other way? Most people answer that question by trying harder to be good. That answer is wrong, and correcting it is the reason this article exists. A son or daughter of God does not produce a holy life by effort. A son or daughter of God yields to Christ and lets Christ produce the holy life from the inside. That is the whole secret, and the rest of this article is simply Scripture saying it.

## The branch does not make the fruit

The Lord Jesus gives the picture Himself, and He defines every part of it as He speaks:

> Abide in Me, and I in you. As the branch cannot bear fruit of itself, except it abide in the vine; no more can ye, except ye abide in Me. I am the vine, ye are the branches: He that abideth in Me, and I in him, the same bringeth forth much fruit: for without Me ye can do nothing.
>
> — John 15:4–5 KJV

The branch does not strain to manufacture grapes. The branch simply stays joined to the vine, and the life of the vine produces the fruit through the branch. "Without Me ye can do nothing" — not a little, but nothing. This is the difference between religion and the gospel. Religion tells a person to become better by effort. The gospel tells a person that the only life that pleases God is God's own life, lived out through the person. Paul says the same thing about his own life:

> I am crucified with Christ: nevertheless I live; yet not I, but Christ liveth in me: and the life which I now live in the flesh I live by the faith of the Son of God, who loved me, and gave Himself for me.
>
> — Galatians 2:20 KJV

"Not I, but Christ." That is the entire secret. It is why Paul could spend the whole of Romans 7 describing the exhausting failure of self-effort — the harder Paul tried in his own strength, the more he lost — and then open Romans 8 with relief:

> For the law of the Spirit of life in Christ Jesus hath made me free from the law of sin and death.
>
> — Romans 8:2 KJV

## The fruit is the Spirit's, not the believer's

And so the fruit, when it finally comes, is not the believer's own fruit at all. Look carefully at what Scripture calls it:

> But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, longsuffering, gentleness, goodness, faith, meekness, temperance.
>
> — Galatians 5:22–23 KJV

It is the fruit of *the Spirit* — the Spirit's produce, not the believer's. And the glory for that fruit goes where the work was actually done:

> Being filled with the fruits of righteousness, which are by Jesus Christ, unto the glory and praise of God.
>
> — Philippians 1:11 KJV

This is why God does not simply hand a son or daughter more willpower. God knows what is in a human being, and God does not trust it:

> But Jesus did not commit Himself unto them, because He knew all men, and needed not that any should testify of man: for He knew what was in man.
>
> — John 2:24–25 KJV

So God does not give a son or daughter of God superpowers, to go and be impressive on His behalf. God gives something far more humbling and far more glorious. God gives Himself, to live inside the believer:

> And I will put My Spirit within you, and cause you to walk in My statutes, and ye shall keep My judgments, and do them.
>
> — Ezekiel 36:27 KJV

"I will cause you to walk." God does not say, "you will manage it on your own." This is the hope that was hidden for ages and is now made plain:

> To whom God would make known what is the riches of the glory of this mystery among the Gentiles; which is Christ in you, the hope of glory.
>
> — Colossians 1:27 KJV

> **A better human is not the same as Christ living in you. One is your effort. The other is His life.**

Keep that distinction, because the next part of the system depends on it. Many saved people are sincerely trying to become better human beings, and that is a real and good thing. But becoming a better human being is not the same as letting Christ Himself bear His own fruit through a yielded son or daughter, for His own glory. The one produces an improved version of a person. The other produces Christ.

## All those commands are one law of love

An honest reader will raise an objection right here. The letters of Peter, of James, of John, and of Paul are full of commands. Be holy. Be kind. Put away anger. Speak the truth. Forgive one another. Be patient. The whole of the New Testament can read like a long list of things a believer is supposed to go and do — and that sounds like effort all over again, the very thing this article has been taking apart. So the objection has to be answered plainly: are all of those commands a to-do list that a son or daughter of God must now accomplish by trying hard?

They are not, and the reason is that the Lord Jesus reduced the entire list to a single commandment:

> A new commandment I give unto you, That ye love one another; as I have loved you, that ye also love one another.
>
> — John 13:34 KJV

One commandment. Not a hundred. Love one another. Paul gives that one commandment its name, calling it the law of Christ, and Paul says the whole of the rest of the law is already contained inside it:

> Bear ye one another's burdens, and so fulfil the law of Christ.
>
> — Galatians 6:2 KJV

> For all the law is fulfilled in one word, even in this; Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself.
>
> — Galatians 5:14 KJV

> He that loveth another hath fulfilled the law… love is the fulfilling of the law.
>
> — Romans 13:8, 10 KJV

So the many commands are not a new pile of duties stacked on top of love. The many commands are the single law of love, written out in detail so that a son or daughter of God can recognize what love actually looks like when it meets a real situation. To be kind is what love does. To forgive is what love does. To speak the truth, to be patient, to put away anger — each command is simply love, described in one particular moment. There is one law, and the name of that one law is love.

And this is exactly why this article has been speaking of the fruit of the Spirit. Look again at the very first word of that fruit:

> But the fruit of the Spirit is love…
>
> — Galatians 5:22 KJV

The one law is love, and the first fruit of the Spirit is love, because the law and the fruit are the same thing seen from two sides. The law of love is what God requires; the fruit of love is what Christ produces. So the one law is not kept by a son or daughter of God gritting their teeth and working down the apostles' list. The one law is kept by Christ Himself, living inside the believer and loving through the believer by His own Spirit. This is the law Paul named at the start of this article — "the law of the Spirit of life in Christ Jesus" (Romans 8:2). Christ keeps that law. And that means something stronger than Christ merely helping a son or daughter of God to obey it. Christ does not stand beside the believer and assist the believer's own effort at love. Christ Himself, living inside the believer, is the One doing the loving. So the son or daughter of God yields, and the love that then fulfills every command is not the believer's love that Christ has improved — the love is Christ's own love, poured out through that son or daughter. The obedience itself is His.

## What this sets up

This is how a son or daughter of God lives a life that pleases God: not by effort, but by yielding to Christ and letting Christ live. And it is this yielded life — the life in which Christ bears His own fruit through a son or daughter of God — that the next part of the system rewards. The reward is the subject of the next article.
