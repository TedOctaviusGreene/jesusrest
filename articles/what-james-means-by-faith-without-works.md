# What James Means by Faith Without Works

*The hardest objection to the whole system — and how, read in its own context, James proves the system instead of breaking it.*

**The Salvation System — Part 6 of Eight**

This article is about one part of a single, connected system of salvation, and it answers the hardest objection raised against the whole system: the words of James, "faith without works is dead." Here is the whole system in one sentence, so that this part can be seen in its place: God's salvation is a gift that a person cannot earn and cannot lose; the person who receives that gift becomes a son or a daughter of God; a son or daughter who lives by the flesh is disciplined but never disowned; a son or daughter who yields to Christ bears Christ's own fruit and is rewarded for it; and at no point in the entire system can anyone boast.

More people try to break this system with the letter of James than with any other passage in the Bible. The argument runs like this: James says that faith without works is dead, therefore a person whose faith does not produce the right works is not really saved. This article shows that the argument misreads James. Read in the context James was actually writing in, and about the thing James was actually addressing, James does not break the system. James confirms it. In James, a faith that works earns a reward, and a faith that is dead earns discipline — and salvation itself, which comes by faith, stands the whole time.

## "Can faith save him?" — saved from what?

Start with the verse itself, and read it as a real question:

> What doth it profit, my brethren, though a man say he hath faith, and have not works? can faith save him?
>
> — James 2:14 KJV

"Can faith save him?" Save him from *what?* That single question decides everything, and James answers it himself by naming who he is talking to — "my brethren." James is not writing to strangers wondering how to get into heaven. James is writing to people who are already in the family. And their salvation was never in their works to begin with; it was the free gift, received by faith, sealed and impossible to lose. So whatever James means by "save," James cannot mean "keep them out of hell" — that was finished the day they believed. James means the one thing a wandering son or daughter really does face. A dead faith that produces nothing cannot save a son or daughter from the Father's discipline. That is what a dead faith cannot spare you. Not your salvation — your correction.

## What a living faith earns

The discipline, though, is the smaller half and the lesser warning. The thing James is actually driving at is the other side — what a *living* faith earns. Watch where James goes:

> Was not Abraham our father justified by works, when he had offered Isaac his son upon the altar?… And the scripture was fulfilled which saith, Abraham believed God, and it was imputed unto him for righteousness: and he was called the Friend of God.
>
> — James 2:21, 23 KJV

Read the two things Abraham received, because they are not the same thing. First, Abraham's faith "was imputed unto him for righteousness" — that is the gift, the standing, credited to Abraham in Genesis 15, long before Isaac was ever on the altar. That standing never came from works. But then, when that same faith produced the works — when Abraham obeyed and offered his son — Abraham "was called the Friend of God." That is a title. That is an honor. That is a reward. The faith that *worked* earned Abraham a name that Abraham would not otherwise have carried. Abraham's righteousness was the gift. Abraham's friendship was the reward.

That is what James means when James says a man is justified by works:

> Ye see then how that by works a man is justified, and not by faith only.
>
> — James 2:24 KJV

James does not mean justified *unto salvation* by works — that would flatly contradict Paul, and Scripture does not contradict itself. James means justified in the sense of *vindicated and rewarded*: God honors the faith God sees bear fruit, and God crowns that faith with a reward. A faith with works is justified *with* a reward. A faith without works is dead — and a dead faith, in a son or daughter of God, gathers only the discipline described earlier in this system. There is the whole of James, and it is exactly the system: faith receives the righteousness, the works of that faith gather the reward, and a faith that does nothing gathers only the Father's hand.

## Two courtrooms, not a contradiction

This also clears up the old puzzle that troubles people most. James says a man is "justified by works," while Paul says a man is "justified by faith without the deeds of the law" (Romans 3:28). Those two statements look like a flat contradiction, but they are not, because the two men are speaking of two different courtrooms. Paul speaks of being justified *before God*: how a guilty sinner is declared righteous in heaven, which is by faith alone, forever and only — the gift. James speaks of being justified *before men*: how a faith is demonstrated, proven real, and so honored — and it is this demonstrated faith that God rewards.

That is why the two men reach for the same Abraham at two different moments. Paul reaches for Genesis 15, where Abraham's faith is first credited as righteousness. James reaches for Genesis 22, decades later, where that same faith is proven on the altar and crowned with a name. One moment is the verdict that saves. The other moment is the vindication that is rewarded.

## Even the reward gives no room to boast

And to keep even the reward from becoming a reason to boast, ask the question this whole system keeps asking: when a living faith does its works, who is doing the working?

> For it is God which worketh in you both to will and to do of His good pleasure.
>
> — Philippians 2:13 KJV

The works that earn the reward are God's works, done in the believer, by God's good pleasure. So even here, at the very place where the reward is gathered, no son or daughter of God can stand up and take the credit. The righteousness was a gift. The reward was Christ working through a yielded son or daughter. From end to end, there is nothing left to boast about.

So the passage most often used to break the system turns out to hold the system together. Faith gives the righteousness, freely. The works of that faith gather a reward. A dead faith gathers only the Father's discipline. And salvation, which comes by faith, is never in question through any of it. Every part of the system has now been laid out. What remains is to set all the parts side by side and show that they hold together as one whole, which is the subject of the next article.
